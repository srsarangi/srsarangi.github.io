How to prepare for a Ph.d interview

One of the most common questions that I am asked is how to get admitted to the PhD program at one of the IITs or other premier institutes. I often do not have an answer because the answer is very subjective. Different IITs have different admission criteria, which keep evolving over time. Even in my department (computer science, IIT Delhi) every year we try to improve the system, which has an effect on the duration and the formats of the exam. Instead of answering this question, let me try to answer this question indirectly by saying what I would have done if I were to apply to any of the IITs for a PhD.

Let me start with a disclaimer. The content that I am writing in this article exclusively represents my personal views. It does not represent nor claim to represent the views of the computer science department at IIT Delhi, or of IIT Delhi in general. Neither am I claiming that by reading this article your chances of getting through a PhD interview increase. I am simply putting my views forward from my perspective.
Let us now look at a PhD in some more detail. It is widely considered the last and highest degree in a student's educational journey. Some places have an additional degree called DSc; however, this is more of an exception than a norm. Clearly, a PhD is like the top of the pyramind -- the apex. As I have argued some of my other articles, PhD is also a license to teach, conduct research, and engage in high end R&D activities. Secondly, it is unlike any other degree. Other degrees are time based and a student needs to clear a minimum number of credits to obtain a degree. However, a PhD requires the student to create something new, innovative, and technically substantive. The program is not time bound, and it is not even guaranteed that all the candidates entering the PhD program will leave the program with a degree! The take home point here is that PhD is an advanced degree, and thus an interview for admission to a PhD program will also be difficult.
Let me try to quantify this difficulty. Most IITs publicly release their shortlisting criteria months before the application deadline. The shortlisting criteria specify a minimum GPA (or percentage) requirement. These criteria often filter out a large number of potential candidates. Subsequently, there is an interview process, which itself might have multiple stages. The first stage is typically a written exam, where the candidate needs to answer a couple of questions (both objective and subjective). There might be additional written rounds also. Subsequently, there are a couple of oral exams, where the candidate is evaluated by a committee of faculty members. The first round might consist of a smaller committee of maybe 3-4 faculty members. In IIT Delhi the last round consists of an interview by a committee that consists of all the faculty members in the department (typically 20-30). As a result the acceptance rates are not very high in most premier institutes (1/30 to 1/60 to the best of my knowledge).
